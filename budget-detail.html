Detailed budget breakdown

The working deep-dive — revenue and spending, line by line, year by year. This is where we hunt for bloat, unwarranted growth, and the COVID-money question. A work in progress; corrections and tips welcome on the Get Involved page.

The big picture

General Fund grew ~30% in six years; population stayed flat

Adopted General Fund spending rose from $19.9M (FY2019-20) to ~$26.98M (FY2025-26) — about +29.6% — while county population held near 12,300. Property tax (now >half of revenue) and a recurring fund-balance draw (~$2M/yr) carried the gap. The tables below break that down; the question is which increases are justified and which are bloat.

All figures are from the county's adopted budget line-item reports (Tyler/Munis), FY2016-17 → FY2025-26. "Requested" columns (FY25-26 spending) are pre-adoption estimates. Cross-check anything important against the source PDFs on the Documents page.
The headline

Property tax: gentle climb, then the FY2026-27 jump

Property-tax revenue rose modestly for years — roughly tracking inflation — and the county is even lowering the rate from 64.5¢ to a proposed 52.5¢. But the 2026 revaluation sharply raised valuations — the revenue-neutral rate (43.5¢ vs the old 64.5¢) implies the tax base grew by roughly half on average (many homes more) — so that "lower" rate collects far MORE. The green bar is the revenue-neutral rate (43.5¢, which holds revenue flat); the red bar is the proposed 52.5¢ — a big jump, not a normalization.

Toggle Inflation-adjusted and you'll see real property tax has been roughly flat (even dipping) for years — which makes the proposed FY2026-27 jump stand out even more. The COVID-money question

Where the ARPA / federal pandemic money went

The pandemic money rose from $0 to a roughly $2.9M peak in FY2022-23, then fell back to ~$0 — no standalone "COVID program" survived. The subtler move: the county took its ~$2.47M of ARPA as "revenue replacement" and spent it covering ordinary department salaries for one year. That freed up an equal amount of local money, which was banked in fund balance — and is now being drawn down ~$2M/year. That parked savings is the through-line from COVID relief to today's "we need a windfall."

Watch the blue line against the bars: federal COVID money vanishes after FY2022-23, but the fund-balance "savings" draw stays elevated and peaks in FY2023-24 — the year after (red dot). ARPA didn't cut anyone's taxes (property & sales held steady); it was banked, and the structural savings-draw it papered over kept right on going. CARES grants peaked FY2021-22; ARPA closed out by FY2025. Per-year totals are estimates from the budget reports + FY2025 audit.

Where the FY2022-23 ARPA actually went: normal salaries

The mechanism: when federal dollars pay this year's salaries, local tax dollars are freed up — and here they were banked, not returned to taxpayers. When the ARPA ran out, the payroll stayed on the local levy and the banked savings began draining. Which positions were ARPA-seeded and are now locally funded? — a top investigation lead. The core question

Does the county actually need this windfall?

Here's the mechanism that should reassure taxpayers — and the reason the windfall is hard to justify. Property-tax revenue = tax base × rate. When new homes are built, their value is added to the tax base, so at the same rate the county automatically collects more. NC law even names this the "growth factor" (G.S. 159-11(e)): the average annual increase in the tax base from new construction and improvements.

So: if Pamlico has had healthy new-home construction while population stayed roughly flat, property-tax revenue should already be rising at the existing 64.5¢ rate — no hike required. Raising the rate on top of new-construction growth and a revaluation is a compounded windfall, unless it's matched by genuine new cost. to quantify next

To prove it with numbers (coming to this page): total taxable value by year (separating reval appreciation from new construction), building-permit counts, and new-parcel counts — all public records (Tax Admin 252-745-3105; permits).

Myth-check

The "state-mandated services" excuse

Officials lean on "state-mandated services" to explain a growing budget. The problem: the mandates haven't changed.

  • The set of NC county mandates — sheriff, jail, courts, public-school capital, social services, public health, register of deeds, elections, tax assessment — has been stable for decades. There is no major new mandate in the last ~10 years. (UNC School of Government)
  • Social Services is mostly federal/state funded — the local taxpayer's share is largely administration, not the benefits (Medicaid, SNAP, Work First). Even the NC Association of County Commissioners argues mandated costs should be paid by state/federal revenue, not local property tax. (NCDHHS)
  • Fire protection is NOT a state mandate — in Pamlico it's funded by separate fire-district taxes, not the county general fund.
  • The real recent cost shift — the 2025 federal change pushing SNAP administration costs onto counties — is brand new and doesn't take effect until October 2026, so it can't explain a budget built up before it existed. Medicaid expansion's local cost is paid by a hospital assessment, not the county.
Bottom line: the mandates didn't change — spending choices did. The genuinely discretionary part of the budget (salaries, raises, capital, fund-balance targets) is where commissioners decide, and where "mandates" can't take the blame.
Context

Why property tax — and the Florida/Texas myth

Pamlico gets >half its general fund from property tax. That's normal in NC, not a scandal — but it also points to the honest alternative officials skipped.

  • 57 of 100 NC counties rely on property tax for at least half their general fund; statewide it's ~56–61% of county revenue. NC law gives counties essentially two broad revenue tools — property tax and local sales tax, so rural counties lean on property tax structurally. (NCACC; Tax Foundation)
  • Counties that lean less on property tax have big sales-tax or tourism/occupancy bases (beach towns, urban counties). That's exactly why Pamlico's own commissioners floated a 0.25% local-option sales tax — the honest way to broaden the base instead of just raising the rate.
  • The "Texas/Florida have low property taxes" idea is mostly a myth. Texas has among the highest property taxes in the country (~1.60% effective, no income tax). Florida taxes property too (~0.86%), softened by a homestead exemption and a 3%/yr assessment cap, while leaning on tourism. There's no free lunch — states without one tax lean harder on another.
  • NC actually keeps local property taxes relatively low (~0.78% effective, below Texas) — largely because the state pays for school operations (teacher salaries) and NC has an income tax, while TX/FL push school operating costs onto local property tax.
Takeaway: high property-tax reliance is structural and normal — but when the base jumps in a revaluation, the law requires publishing a revenue-neutral rate. Adopting anything higher is a tax increase, even if the printed rate looks lower. The fair move is revenue-neutral plus a transparent case for any specific new need.

The Sheriff question

Did doubling the Sheriff's budget make us safer?

A fair question — and not a knock on the deputies; it's about the appropriation the commissioners set, not the work. The Sheriff's Office (+72%), the Detention Center (+55%) and 911 Dispatch (+113%) all climbed since FY2016-17 — together from ~$3.7M to ~$6.2M — while county population slowly declined. What did taxpayers get for it? Not a response to rising crime — there wasn't any. Reported offenses fell ~59% from their 2013 peak to a 15-year low in 2024. Whatever drove that decline (property crime has fallen regionally too), a shrinking crime count is not a justification for law-enforcement spending that grew this fast.

Reported index offenses per year (blue bars) vs. the Sheriff + Jail/Detention + 911 Dispatch adopted budget (red line, $M). Crime fell from a 2013 peak of 358 offenses to 148 in 2024 — its lowest in the 15+ years of data — down ~59%, while that combined public-safety spending rose from ~$2.90M (FY2014-15) to ~$6.17M (FY2024-25), up ~113%. The likely real drivers — staffing a near-full jail, wages/benefits, inflation — are budget choices, and belong in the line-item review, not behind a "public safety" headline.
So what did it cost per crime? Run it honestly. Sheriff + Jail + Dispatch spending rose from ~$2.90M (FY2014-15) to ~$6.17M (FY2024-25) — about +$3.27M a year (~113%) — while reported offenses fell from the 2013 peak of 358 to 148. Credit the budget for the whole ~210-crime drop and you get roughly $15,600 per crime avoided, per year. But you can't credit it: spending rose and crime fell together for a decade, and property crime fell statewide over the same span — so the decline isn't something the budget can claim. And it matters where the money went (chart below): ~96% of the growth is salaries and benefits, operating costs barely moved, and part of the jail's growth is a federal-inmate housing contract — a revenue operation, not local crime-fighting. The verdict: a budget that doubled, a crime decline it can't take credit for, and no documented link between the two.

Where did the money go? People, not equipment.

Sheriff + Jail + Dispatch budget split into Personnel (salaries, overtime & benefits), Operating (jail food/medical, fuel, uniforms, supplies, contracts), Capital (vehicles/equipment) and lease debt, FY2015-16 → FY2024-25. Personnel is the whole story: it drove ~96% of the growth ($2.27M → $5.07M) and is ~82% of the FY24-25 total; operating is mostly jail food & medical, capital is sporadic, and lease debt has fallen to zero. The reports lump all sworn and civilian pay into single salary codes — so patrol-deputy vs. administrative headcount isn't in the budget; that split lives in the county's position/pay plan (a clean records request). FY2025-26 omitted — the posted draft left personnel lines blank. Source: county Munis expense reports (cost centers 4310/4320/4321).

The jail is partly a business. The FY2025-26 budget message confirms the Detention Center (108 beds, ~105 average daily) houses 40–60 federal inmates a day at ~$80 each — about $1.1M a year in revenue. So part of the jail's budget growth is a revenue-generating boarding operation, not local crime control, and its costs (inmate food, medical, staffing a near-full facility) rise with the contract. The question worth tracking: does the housing revenue still cover the cost as the operation grows? Source: FY2025-26 budget message.
