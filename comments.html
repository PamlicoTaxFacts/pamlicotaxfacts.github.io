The tax rate

Rate & revenue-neutral

🟥 Proposed rate · #

The current proposed tax rate is not 52.5 cents — it is 50.5 cents.

Our response — confirmed close to this: the June budget meeting put the proposal at 50.75¢ (revenue-neutral ~43.75¢) — essentially your number. The report now uses 50.75¢ throughout (still "as reported," pending the official minutes; the last adopted rate is 64.5¢). Thank you — this sharpened the whole site.
🟩 Revenue-neutral rate · #

The revenue-neutral rate has increased to 43.75 cents — related to appeals, including ~$12,000,000 in appeals protection. If the calculation is accurate, true neutral is higher than 43.5¢.

Our response: Confirmed in principle — revenue-neutral does rise as appeals shrink the base. We corrected the report and now use this to argue for taking the one-year delay until the true base (and true neutral rate) is known.
🟥 Was revenue-neutral discussed? · #

Revenue-neutral was discussed — at the first budget meeting. The County Compass article came after the second meeting. At that first meeting the Board indicated revenue-neutral was not feasible, citing the prior-year deficit, the school-loan payment, and jail-revenue concerns.

Our response: Taken — we removed the "staff didn't discuss revenue-neutral" framing from the report.
🟦 The prior revaluation · #

During the prior revaluation the County did not go revenue-neutral — per the budget message it adopted a rate about 5 cents above neutral (an increase of roughly 2 cents).

Our response: Half-confirmed. The FY2020-21 message does show 62.5¢ vs a 57.5¢ neutral (5¢ above). But the rate did not rise 2¢ — it was held flat at 62.5¢; it only sat above neutral because the reval raised values. That's the same phantom-windfall mechanism as now — we added it to the windfall section.
🟦 Real revenue growth · #

Property values grew only ~2%/yr; since property tax funds ~half of costs, that revenue grew only ~10% over six years — while inflation was high.

Our response: Reasonable and consistent with our finding that real per-resident spending is roughly flat — which is exactly why a ~16% windfall is hard to justify.
Financial condition

Deficits & reserves

🟥 Has the county run deficits? · #

The claim that the County has not had recent deficits is likely incorrect. A six-year presentation (including ARPA) indicated the County went negative even with ARPA. It carries a deficit of ~$500,000 from the prior year ($467,000 confirmed). The school loan had no payment last year; a worst-case ~$900,000 was described.

Our response — verified, with a correction: NC counties can't run an unlawful deficit (G.S. 159-8). What the audits show is reserve drawdowns: FY2024 −$504K and FY2025 −$467K — your "$500K" and "$467K" are two different years, not one. Reserves grew through ARPA, then dipped. We refined "no deficit" and added a fund-balance chart.
🟦 How much reserve is prudent? · #

The County should keep a fund balance of at least 25% — property tax is cyclical (due Jan 5), so it may need ~six months of expenses; plus disaster risk (Hurricane Florence nearly bankrupted it). Fund balance was near 50% in 2020 and ~33% now — flexibility has been lost.

Our response — agree, with a correction: We agree 25%+ is prudent and now say so. But the numbers: available reserves were ~28.6% in 2020 (the ~50% figure is total fund balance), rose to a 39.6% peak in 2023, and are 33.3% now — they rose then dipped, not a steady decline. See the fund-balance chart.
🟦 Budgeted vs. used fund balance · #

Budgeting a fund-balance draw isn't the same as spending it. Review audited actuals, not just budgeted figures.

Our response: Agreed and adopted — our fund-balance chart uses audited actuals (net change in fund balance), not the budgeted appropriation.
Where the money goes

How to read the spending

🟩 Net cost of services · #

Total appropriations mislead — many departments are offset by restricted/program revenue. The real burden is the net. The big net-cost functions are the Sheriff, schools, and Rescue Squad; cut efforts should focus there, not on departments covered by dedicated revenue.

Our response — adopted: This is one of the most useful points we received. We added a "net cost of services" chart — Human Services is large but mostly state/federal money, while schools, the Sheriff, and the Rescue Squad are the real property-tax burden.
🟩 Actuals vs. budget · #

The Sheriff's Office appears to have exceeded its original budget in multiple years — so its true cost is higher than the adopted budget alone suggests.

Our response: Flagged as a records-request lead in the Sheriff section (the year-by-year actual-vs-budget split needs the county's own reports).
🟥 What the new spending buys · #

The budget doesn't raise spending merely to draw down reserves — the Manager proposes specific additions: courthouse-security deputies, an IT position, a Health Educator, an Income Maintenance Worker, and a housekeeper (compliance/service needs).

Our response: Fair that these are specific — but specific isn't the same as necessary. We present the stated justifications and still ask whether each is essential, since "we added real positions" can still be discretionary growth.
🟦 Capital investment · #

The County has under-invested in capital (per the State Treasurer baseline) and is adding capital funding so future needs stop drawing down reserves; consolidating buildings/IT/fleet to track true cost.

Our response: Capital needs are real and tracking true cost is sensible. But rebuilding capital reserves via a permanent tax windfall isn't the only path — reserves, one-time funds, and cost control can do it without locking in higher taxes forever.
🟦 State-mandated cost increases · #

Health-insurance and retirement costs rose sharply due to state law — an extra 2.4% employer retirement contribution (~11%) and a ~15% health-insurance increase.

Our response — partly confirmed: The LGERS pension increase is real and state-set (we credit it). But the ~15% is the State Health Plan (teachers/state employees) — county employees aren't on it, so that figure isn't theirs. We refined the mandates section: no new mandated services, but some mandated costs did rise.
Specific functions

Where the scrutiny belongs

🟩 The Sheriff's Office · #

Per the State Treasurer's AFIR data, the Sheriff's Office spends ~$1,000,000 more than similarly-sized counties, and Jones County appears to have about half the staffing of Pamlico's. Why nearly double?

Our response — confirmed, with nuance: The ~$1M (public-safety, AFIR) checks out, and Pamlico does have ~2× Jones' total staff — but on sworn deputies they're about even (≈26 vs 22). The gap is jail + dispatch civilians, not patrol. We separated the jail (a federal-inmate revenue business) and refocused on the Sheriff's own +122% personnel growth.
🟩 The Rescue Squad · #

The Rescue Squad (a non-profit) requested almost 30% more over last year; the County may pay nearly $1.4M while the Squad collects ~$900K. One paramedic reportedly earned over $100,000.

Our response: Confirmed as one of the largest net (local-dollar) costs — the County's ~$1.4M is nearly all local money (EMS billing goes to the Squad, not the County). Flagged in the net-cost section.
🟦 Where real cuts would have to come · #

Meaningful cuts would have to come from the largest, most politically sensitive areas — schools and the Sheriff's Office; roughly $2,000,000 combined, though that would likely require a reduction in force.

Our response: Consistent with our net-cost finding — the leverage is in the big local-dollar functions, not the grant-funded ones.
Population & data

Getting the denominators right

🟥 Population correction · #

The site used the 2020 Census (~12,276), which is ~6 years old. The NC State Demographer's certified estimate shows the population grew ~5.5% to 12,990 — which could change per-capita spending and funding figures.

Our response — confirmed, and corrected: You're right. OSBM certified 12,990 (2024), up ~5.8% since 2020 — the county is growing, not shrinking. We fixed every per-resident chart to the correct (growing) population. Honest consequence: real per-resident total spending is roughly flat, so we dropped "spending exploded per person" — the case is now about the mix (jail + overhead) and the windfall.
🟦 "8th of 100" on local appropriations · #

Why is Pamlico ranked 8th of 100 counties on total local appropriations? Isn't that a drag on our tax rate?

Our response — number's right, conclusion isn't: Pamlico is 8th on the Public School Forum's Total Local Appropriations per student — but that's a small-district math artifact (≈1,150 students) plus ~$1.86M of state small-county/low-wealth aid baked into the "total." On actual effort relative to ability to pay, Pamlico is 53rd — middle of the pack. Schools are a big local cost (our net-cost point), but this ranking isn't proof they uniquely inflate the rate.
