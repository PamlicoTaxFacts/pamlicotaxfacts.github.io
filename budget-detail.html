Detailed budget breakdown

The working deep-dive — revenue and spending, line by line, year by year. This is where we hunt for bloat, unwarranted growth, and the COVID-money question. A work in progress; corrections and tips welcome on the Get Involved page.

📄 Budget Documents & Sources   ← Full report

From around 2022, the Commission appears to have used ARPA money as much to expand ongoing departments as to cover real emergencies. The federal cash is gone, but that spending stayed in the budget, and the county now wants a permanent ~19% property-tax increase to keep paying for it. The revaluation it rests on is regressive (it over-assesses the cheapest homes), and it's fair to ask why a 6-to-1 Republican board, in a slow-growing and aging county, is overseeing this. The charts below are the facts — take your time and make up your own mind.

The big picture

General Fund grew ~30% in six years; population stayed flat

Adopted General Fund spending rose from $19.9M (FY2019-20) to ~$26.98M (FY2025-26) — about +29.6% — while county population held near 12,300. Property tax (now >half of revenue) and a recurring fund-balance draw (~$2M/yr) carried the gap. The tables below break that down; the question is which increases are justified and which are bloat.

All figures are from the county's adopted budget line-item reports (Tyler/Munis), FY2016-17 → FY2025-26. The headline

Property tax: gentle climb, then the FY2026-27 jump

Property-tax revenue rose modestly for years — roughly tracking inflation — and the county is even lowering the rate from 64.5¢ to a proposed 52.0¢. But the 2026 revaluation sharply raised valuations — the revenue-neutral rate (~43.75¢ vs the old 64.5¢) implies the tax base grew by roughly half on average (many homes more) — so that "lower" rate collects far MORE. The green bar is the revenue-neutral rate (~43.75¢, which holds revenue flat); the red bar is the proposed 52.0¢ — a big jump, not a normalization.

Toggle Inflation-adjusted and you'll see real property tax has been roughly flat (even dipping) for years — which makes the proposed FY2026-27 jump stand out even more. Is it fair?

Regressive: the revaluation hit cheaper homes hardest

Is this a "tax the millionaires" move, or does it land hardest on modest homes? Two independent measures say the same thing — cheaper homes got the worse end. First, the test assessors are graded on (the line): each home's 2026 assessed value vs. what it actually sold for (1,146 arm's-length sales, 2022-2026). A fair system assesses every home near 100% of market; Pamlico assesses homes under $100K at ~116% of market but homes over $350K at ~99%. Second, the raw valuation jump (the columns): we pulled the prior (2020) value off the county's own property cards for a random 239 homes — the cheapest homes were raised a median +82% since 2020, the most expensive only +49%. Same curve, from raw public data.

Line — median assessed ÷ sale-price (arm's-length sales with a building, 2022-2026; n=1,146). 1.00 = assessed at market; above 1.00 = over-assessed. Textbook regressivity: the IAAO "price-related differential" is 1.14 (over 1.03 is flagged regressive), and it holds on 2025-26 sales alone, so it isn't just market timing. Columns — median actual increase from the 2020 value to the 2026 value, read off the county's own Property Record Cards for a random sample of 239 homes (bucketed by 2020 value; boat slips, vacant-land splits, and new construction excluded). For reference, the whole county's taxable base rose only ~48% — so homes (median ~+72%) were revalued far harder than commercial and personal property (boats, vehicles). Source: county 2026 parcel file, recorded sale prices, and PRC cards (our calculation).

What it means: a windfall taken on these assessments falls hardest, proportionally, on lower-value homeowners — the opposite of progressive. And since wealthier owners are likelier to file (and win) appeals, the burden tilts further toward modest homes. This is a regressive tax increase, not an egalitarian one.

How to read the spending

Gross spending ≠ tax burden: the "net cost" of each function

A department's total budget can mislead, because many are largely paid by restricted state/federal/fee revenue, not local property tax. The real burden on your tax bill is the net — what county dollars must cover. Pamlico took in ~$11.3M of intergovernmental revenue (FY2024), most tied to Human Services — so that big-looking budget is mostly not on local taxpayers.

Each function's spending (blue) next to its own outside revenue (green) — state/federal aid, jail fees, permits. The gap between the two is what local property tax covers. Education and the Sheriff have almost no green (all property tax); Human Services is mostly green (state/federal aid does the heavy lifting); the Jail's federal-inmate fees cover ~half its cost. FY2024-25; matched revenue mapped from county revenue lines (approximate — exact per-department offsets need the county line-item budget). Source: county budget reports.

Why it matters: if the goal is to lower the tax rate, the leverage is in the big net-cost functions — schools, the Sheriff, the Rescue Squad — not departments like Social Services whose large budgets are mostly other people's money.

The COVID-money question

Where the ARPA / federal pandemic money went

The pandemic money rose from $0 to a roughly $2.9M peak in FY2022-23, then fell back to ~$0 — no standalone "COVID program" survived. The subtler move: the county took its ~$2.47M of ARPA as "revenue replacement" and spent it covering ordinary department salaries for one year. That freed up an equal amount of local money, which was banked in fund balance — and is now being drawn down ~$2M/year. That parked savings is the through-line from COVID relief to today's "we need a windfall."

Watch the blue line against the bars: federal COVID money vanishes after FY2022-23, but the fund-balance "savings" draw stays elevated and peaks in FY2023-24 — the year after (red dot). ARPA didn't cut anyone's taxes (property & sales held steady); it was banked, and the structural savings-draw it papered over kept right on going. CARES grants peaked FY2021-22; ARPA closed out by FY2025. Per-year totals are estimates from the budget reports + FY2025 audit.

Reserves

Built up on ARPA, now drawing down — but still healthy

Can a county even run a "deficit"? Not really — NC law requires a balanced budget (G.S. 159-8); the county can't spend money it doesn't have or borrow for operations. What it can do is draw down savings. The record: reserves grew every year FY2020-2023 (helped by banked ARPA), then dipped in FY2024 and FY2025 (−$504K, −$467K) — small reserve draws, not a crisis.

Bars: net change in General-Fund balance each year (green = added to savings, red = drew down). Line: available (unassigned) reserves as % of spending — the LGC's key health metric. Reserves rose from 25.7% (2019) to a 39.6% peak (2023), then eased to 33.3% (2025) — still ~4× the LGC's 8% benchmark and about four months of operating costs. Source: Pamlico audited financial statements, FY2019-FY2025.

The point: two modest reserve-draw years don't make a county "broke," and they don't justify a permanent tax windfall. Reserves are exactly what you spend in lean years; you refill them with one-time money and cost control — not by locking in higher taxes forever.
The core question

Does the county actually need this windfall?

Here's the mechanism that should reassure taxpayers — and the reason the windfall is hard to justify. Property-tax revenue = tax base × rate. When new homes are built, their value is added to the tax base, so at the same rate the county automatically collects more. NC law even names this the "growth factor" (G.S. 159-11(e)): the average annual increase in the tax base from new construction and improvements.

So: if Pamlico has had healthy new-home construction while population stayed roughly flat, property-tax revenue should already be rising at the existing 64.5¢ rate — no hike required. Raising the rate on top of new-construction growth and a revaluation is a compounded windfall, unless it's matched by genuine new cost. to quantify next

To prove it with numbers (coming to this page): total taxable value by year (separating reval appreciation from new construction), building-permit counts, and new-parcel counts — all public records (Tax Admin 252-745-3105; permits).

Myth-check

The "state-mandated services" excuse

Officials lean on "state-mandated services" to explain a growing budget. The precise truth: the mandated services haven't changed — though the cost of some has.

  • The set of NC county mandates — sheriff, jail, courts, public-school capital, social services, public health, register of deeds, elections, tax assessment — has been stable for decades. There is no major new mandate in the last ~10 years. (UNC School of Government)
  • Social Services is mostly federal/state funded — the local taxpayer's share is largely administration, not the benefits (Medicaid, SNAP, Work First). But that local share is quietly growing: Pamlico's DSS budget rose ~16% (FY2019-20 → FY2024-25, $2.49M → $2.89M) while state/federal reimbursement stayed essentially flat (~$1.81M) — so the county's own net cost jumped from ~$682K to ~$1.09M (+60%), even though population grew only ~6% and the mandated service didn't change. A flat mandate with a rising local bill is a county staffing/cost decision, not a new state requirement. (NCDHHS; county budgets FY2019-26)
  • Fire protection is NOT a state mandate — in Pamlico it's funded by separate fire-district taxes, not the county general fund.
  • The real recent cost shift — the 2025 federal change pushing SNAP administration costs onto counties — is brand new and doesn't take effect until October 2026, so it can't explain a budget built up before it existed. Medicaid expansion's local cost is paid by a hospital assessment, not the county.
  • What has risen is the cost of the same mandates — but it's smaller than it sounds, and about half self-inflicted. The state-set LGERS pension rate climbed from 9.04% to 13.86% of payroll (FY2020→FY2025), taking the county's pension bill from $548K to $1.17M. Yet only about half of that ~$619K rise is the rate hike itself (~$300-400K); the rest is because the county's covered payroll grew ~39% (to $8.4M) — more positions and raises — while population grew ~6%. Pensions and health insurance are charged per employee, so hiring staff a flat-population county doesn't need multiplies the "mandated" cost — the state sets the rate, the county picks the headcount. And the entire pension-cost increase is only ~2% of the $27M General Fund: real, but dwarfed by discretionary growth (Sheriff +$1.2M, Rescue +$724K). (FY2025 audit, LGERS Schedule of Contributions, p.78)
Bottom line: no new mandated services — and where mandated costs rose (pensions, health, HR1 admin), that's fair to credit. But the genuinely discretionary part (raises, added positions, capital, fund-balance targets) is where commissioners decide — and where "mandates" can't take the blame.
Context

Why property tax — and the Florida/Texas myth

Pamlico gets >half its general fund from property tax. That's normal in NC, not a scandal — but it also points to the honest alternative officials skipped.

  • 57 of 100 NC counties rely on property tax for at least half their general fund; statewide it's ~56–61% of county revenue. NC law gives counties essentially two broad revenue tools — property tax and local sales tax, so rural counties lean on property tax structurally. (NCACC; Tax Foundation)
  • Counties that lean less on property tax have big sales-tax or tourism/occupancy bases (beach towns, urban counties). That's exactly why Pamlico's own commissioners floated a 0.25% local-option sales tax — the honest way to broaden the base instead of just raising the rate.
  • The "Texas/Florida have low property taxes" idea is mostly a myth. Texas has among the highest property taxes in the country (~1.60% effective, no income tax). Florida taxes property too (~0.86%), softened by a homestead exemption and a 3%/yr assessment cap, while leaning on tourism. There's no free lunch — states without one tax lean harder on another.
  • NC actually keeps local property taxes relatively low (~0.78% effective, below Texas) — largely because the state pays for school operations (teacher salaries) and NC has an income tax, while TX/FL push school operating costs onto local property tax.
Takeaway: high property-tax reliance is structural and normal — but when the base jumps in a revaluation, the law requires publishing a revenue-neutral rate. Adopting anything higher is a tax increase, even if the printed rate looks lower. The fair move is revenue-neutral plus a transparent case for any specific new need.

The Sheriff question

The Sheriff's budget: patrol, a jail business, or bloat?

"Public safety doubled" hides three very different things, so here's the honest breakdown. Patrol is not the problem — Pamlico fields about two dozen sworn deputies, comparable to similar counties (Jones runs ~22), and reported crime fell ~59% to a 15-year low. The county also runs a large jail that's mostly a federal-inmate revenue business — a separate question (below). What's left — the Sheriff's own office budget — is the real red flag: its personnel cost more than doubled while patrol stayed flat and crime fell.

The jail is partly a federal-inmate business

Detention Center (cost center 4320) spending, split by who's in the beds, vs. the federal-inmate bed-lease revenue. The county houses roughly 40–60 U.S. Marshals inmates a day out of an average ~105 total (capacity 108) — about 40% of the jail — and the feds pay ~$80 per inmate per day (that's a dollar rate, not a headcount), worth ~$1.1M/yr. We split the purple spending bar by that ~40% / ~60% inmate share — an estimate (it assumes a federal inmate costs the same per day as a local one, and the exact mix isn't published), so don't read the thin year-to-year gaps as a precise profit or loss. The honest read is only directional: the federal share (purple) is roughly matched by the federal fees (green) — that operation broadly pays its own way, it's a revenue program, not waste — while the local/state share (gray) is the mandated cost on county taxpayers, and it's the part that's grown. Most peers run nothing like this (Jones: a 21-bed courthouse-basement lockup; Perquimans: a regional jail), so peer "public-safety" comparisons that don't separate the jail are apples-to-oranges. Sources: FY2025-26 budget message; Prison Policy Initiative.

The honest verdict on the Sheriff. Not over-policing — patrol ≈ peers and crime is down. The big "public safety" number is inflated by a federal-inmate jail business (much of its cost is offset by federal revenue — see the bars above). The real thing to explain is the Sheriff-proper personnel cost more than doubling while patrol stayed flat: that's where to ask "positions or raises?" and pull the pay plan. It's the same overhead-growth pattern as General Government — the place to look, not the deputies on the road.
